Part 01 Messaging Audit

Where TRG Screen's messaging leaves value on the table

Audit across homepage, product pages, and category positioning  ·  May 2026

Current positioning territory

"The leading provider of market data and subscription management technology and automation solutions, tailored to the unique needs of financial institutions and legal firms."

What it could say instead

"750 financial institutions and law firms use TRG Screen to manage $9.6 billion in subscription spend. Not because it's the only tool. Because after one audit, they couldn't go back to spreadsheets."

The problem: The current hero is a category description, not a conviction. "Market data and subscription management technology" tells a buyer what TRG Screen does. It doesn't tell them why they should care right now, or what losing another quarter without it actually costs. The $9.6B number is a genuinely extraordinary proof point sitting in TRG Screen's LinkedIn bio, not on the homepage. For an enterprise with $25M-plus in average subscription spend, the first question isn't "what does this product do?" It's "what does it cost me to keep doing things the way I'm doing them?" The current homepage doesn't answer that question. The entry point should be the pain, not the product category.
Current product narrative (Optimize Spend)

"Optimize Spend 2.0 builds upon its extensive abilities to empower accurate inventory management for current and future periods, and now also for historical periods."

What it could say instead

"Most market data teams don't know exactly what they're paying for today, let alone what they paid two years ago. Optimize Spend 2.0 fixes that: inventory management across past, present, and future periods, with bulk reconciliation built in."

The problem: Optimize Spend won the CODiE award twice in a row. That's not a footnote, that's a headline. The product page buries the "best-in-class" signal in the middle of a capabilities list. More importantly, the 2.0 update adds historical period management, which solves a genuinely painful problem: finance teams during audit season who can't reconstruct what they were paying vendors 18 months ago. That's a real scenario with real consequences. The copy should open with the problem scenario, not the product capability. "Reconstruct your entire subscription history, record by record" is a more motivating opening line than "multi-period reconciliation."
Current managed services framing

Managed services described primarily as invoice processing, inventory management, order fulfillment, and exchange declarations, positioned as operational support.

What it could say instead

"Your market data team wasn't hired to process invoices. Between admin and the work that actually matters, the admin is winning. TRG Screen's managed services handle the operations. Your team gets back to advising on sourcing, challenging demand, and controlling spend before it happens."

The problem: The S4 Market Data partnership (announced November 2025) signals that TRG Screen is moving managed services upstream, from back-office admin to strategic advisory. But the current messaging is still operational. It describes what the service does, not what it gives back. "Invoice processing and order fulfillment" sounds like a cost center. "Getting your team out of the processing loop so they can prevent spend rather than reconcile it" sounds like a business case. The framing needs to move from service features to the professional identity of the person buying it. A market data director doesn't want to be sold admin support. They want to be sold the version of their job that is actually strategic.
Part 02 GTM Brief

GTM Brief  ·  Crizit Acquisition Narrative

Acquired October 2025  ·  Real-time reference data optimization and intelligent data caching

Acquisition GTM

The Context

TRG Screen acquired Crizit in October 2025, a New York-based fintech that does reference data optimization and intelligent data caching for financial institutions. On paper, this extends the platform's capabilities. In practice, it needs a story that current clients and prospects can actually use.

Right now, "we acquired a reference data optimization company" is a press release. The GTM question is: what does it mean for the buyer who's already paying $500K/year in Bloomberg and Refinitiv fees?

The Opportunity

Reference data is one of the least-scrutinized line items in a financial institution's market data budget, partly because it's complex and partly because nobody owns it clearly. Crizit's technology can identify redundant data pulls, stale cache requests, and over-provisioned feeds in real time.

The GTM framing isn't "we added a feature." It's "we can now show you where your data spend is leaking." That's a different conversation with a CTO and a Head of Market Data at the same time.

Target Audiences

Primary: Market data managers at mid-to-large banks who already use Optimize Spend but haven't solved the reference data piece yet. This is an upsell story, not a new-logo story.

Secondary: CTOs and heads of data infrastructure at institutions that haven't engaged TRG Screen before. Crizit is a technology conversation; it opens a door that's currently closed.

Core Claim

The line to test: "You know what you're spending on market data. Do you know what you're wasting on it?"

Secondary angle: The biggest banks spend upwards of $500 million a year on data. A meaningful portion of that goes to feeds nobody is actively using. Crizit identifies those feeds in real time, before the next invoice arrives.

30-60-90 Day Launch Plan

Day 1-30
Don't lead with the acquisition, lead with the problem. Pull three existing clients who've expressed frustration with reference data costs in Gong calls (or similar). Build one case study per client showing what unoptimized reference data was costing them before. Run these as a "State of Reference Data Spend" mini-report shared exclusively with current clients first. No public announcement yet. Create the proof before the pitch.
Day 31-60
Equip sales with two things: a problem statement and a number. The problem statement: "Most banks have optimized their terminal spend. Almost none have optimized their reference data consumption." The number: an estimate (clearly labelled as such) of how much a typical institution with $25M in market data spend might be over-consuming on reference data feeds, based on Crizit's benchmarks. Sales doesn't need a product deck yet. They need one slide that starts a conversation. Build that slide and run an internal enablement session before any external push.
Day 61-90
Go public with the narrative, not the product. Publish a thought leadership piece on "The Reference Data Blind Spot" in partnership with a credible market data publication. The piece should make the case that reference data optimization is the next frontier of market data cost management, cite the three case studies from Day 1-30, and mention Crizit by name only in the final third. Let the problem do the selling, not the product announcement. Follow with a webinar where a Crizit founder walks through a live optimization scenario.

GTM Brief  ·  Optimize Insights Expansion

Ongoing 2025-2026  ·  Transitioning from spend management to actionable intelligence

Platform Expansion

The Shift

TRG Screen's Chief Product Officer described this publicly as a "paradigm shift": moving from helping firms know what they're spending to helping them make better decisions because of that data. Optimize Insights is the product embodiment of that shift.

The messaging problem: "Insights" is what every SaaS company promises. TRG Screen needs to define what insights mean specifically in the market data context, or the word becomes wallpaper.

What "Insights" Actually Means Here

For a Head of Market Data at a bank, an insight isn't a dashboard. It's the moment they walk into a vendor negotiation knowing they've consumed 40% less of a particular data feed than the contract allows, and they can prove it.

The GTM brief for Optimize Insights should be built around three scenarios where having the data changes the outcome of a specific decision: a vendor renewal, a demand challenge, and a compliance audit. Each scenario is a content piece. Each content piece is a sales asset.

Audience Nuance

Market data managers: They want to be seen as strategic by their CFO. Insights give them the language to do that. The message: "Stop reporting what you spent. Start advising on what to spend."

CFOs and Finance leads: They want a number they can act on, not a report they have to interpret. The message: "Here's exactly how much you could have saved in the last quarter if you'd known this before the renewal."

The Competitive Angle

Competitors in this space sell spend visibility. TRG Screen is now selling the layer above that: spend intelligence. The difference in a vendor negotiation between knowing you spent $2M on Bloomberg and knowing you consumed 60% of a specific terminal's capacity is the difference between a conversation and a leverage point.

That distinction needs to be the lead, not a footnote in the feature list.

Part 03 Buyer Map

TRG Screen sells into complex buying committees. The same product lands very differently depending on whether you're talking to a market data director, a CFO, or a compliance officer. Select a buyer type to see how the message should shift.

Select a buyer type above
Buyer: Head of Market Data
"You're expected to advise on sourcing, challenge demand, and prevent spend. That's impossible when you're still processing invoices."
The Head of Market Data is under pressure from two directions at once. From their CFO, they're being asked to control costs on a budget that grows every year as data consumption increases. From their trading desks and research teams, they're being asked to provision more data faster and with less friction. The work that sits between those two pressures, invoice reconciliation, vendor management, exchange declarations, is real work that takes real time. TRG Screen's pitch to this person isn't "buy our software." It's "here's how you stop being the person who processes things and become the person who decides things." The managed services conversation fits here, but only once the platform conversation has established that TRG Screen already understands their specific spend landscape. Lead with the audit. The managed services upsell follows naturally.

Common objections & how to handle them

"We manage our market data spend in-house. We've got it under control." +
This is the most common opening position and it almost never reflects reality fully. The right response isn't to challenge their confidence directly. It's to ask a specific question: "When you say in-house, what does your reconciliation process look like when you get an invoice that doesn't match what you've contracted?" Most teams have a manual process that works most of the time. TRG Screen's value is in the exceptions, and there are always exceptions. The follow-up isn't a product demo. It's a shared audit of one month's worth of invoices. Let the data make the case.
"We're a smaller firm. This feels like it's built for tier-1 banks." +
This objection usually comes from firms spending $5M-$25M on market data annually, which is precisely the segment where the ROI of proper spend management is highest as a percentage of the budget. TRG Screen's 750-client base includes firms well below the tier-1 threshold. The counter here is a peer reference, not a feature list. "Actually, some of our highest-impact deployments have been at firms your size, because that's where the savings as a proportion of spend are most meaningful." Pair it with a case study from a comparable institution if one exists.
"We're mid-contract on our existing data management tools." +
Don't fight the contract timing. Use it. "That's actually a good moment to have this conversation, because the 90 days before your next renewal is when you need the most accurate picture of what you've consumed versus what you contracted. Running an audit now means you walk into that renewal with data instead of assumptions." Turn the contract cycle objection into a reason to start the conversation now. The goal of this call isn't to close a deal. It's to make sure they have what they need before their next negotiation.

Reference data is a different story. The feeds flowing into your systems in the background, the ones powering pricing engines, risk models, and settlement workflows, tend to get renewed on autopilot. Not because nobody cares, but because nobody's had a clean way to see exactly what's being consumed versus what's been contracted.

Why this works: Opens by validating what the reader has already done right (terminal optimization), then introduces the gap without making them feel bad about it. The Crizit technology is introduced as a solution to a named problem, not as a product announcement. The CTA is soft and positions the call as informational, which reduces the barrier to booking it. No "exciting new acquisition" language anywhere.
Sales Enablement  ·  Objection Card  ·  "We use [competitor] already" Sales Card
When they say "we already have a spend management tool"
Don't push back. Ask one question: "Does it handle usage alongside spend, or just the spend side?" Most point solutions do one or the other. The ones that do both often don't handle compliance reporting or managed services. That question creates space without being combative.

If they say their current tool handles both: "Got it. What does your process look like when there's a discrepancy between what you've contracted and what shows up on the invoice?" Most teams have a manual intervention step somewhere. Find that step.

The key message, not to say out loud but to work toward: TRG Screen is the only vendor that handles spend, usage, compliance, and managed services from one provider. The competitive advantage is breadth and the 25+ years of domain depth that makes that breadth trustworthy. That's not a feature comparison conversation. That's a "how much of your time do you want to spend managing multiple vendors" conversation.
Why it works: Most AEs lose competitive deals by defending product capabilities directly. The "does it handle usage alongside spend?" question is genuinely diagnostic, not rhetorical. It either surfaces a gap or leads to a conversation about process, which is where TRG Screen's managed services story naturally enters. The goal of this card is to get the prospect talking about their own workflow, not evaluating feature checklists.
